# Telomerase

**Telomerase** (also known as **terminal transferase** or **TERT**) is a ribonucleoprotein enzyme that adds repetitive nucleotide sequences (TTAGGG in humans) to the ends of eukaryotic chromosomes, called **telomeres**. Without telomerase, telomeres shorten with each cell division, eventually triggering cellular senescence or apoptosis — a limit known as the **Hayflick limit**.

## Biology

- **Composition:** Telomerase consists of two core components:
  - **TERT** (Telomerase Reverse Transcriptase) — the catalytic protein subunit
  - **TERC** (Telomerase RNA Component) — the RNA template that TERT uses to synthesize telomeric DNA
- **Expression:** Active in stem cells, germ cells, and most fetal tissues. **Repressed in most adult somatic cells**.
- **Function:** Replenishes telomeric DNA lost during replication, effectively extending cellular replicative lifespan.

## Relevance to Longevity

Telomere attrition is considered one of the **Hallmarks of Aging**. Restoring telomerase activity has been proposed as a geroprotective strategy:

- **In vitro:** Reactivating telomerase in human somatic cells can extend replicative lifespan beyond the Hayflick limit.
- **Mouse models:** Transgenic telomerase activation or gene therapy has extended lifespan in some engineered mouse strains.
- **Human evidence:** Extremely limited. Small pilot studies with compounds like [[epitalon]] (AEDG) claim telomere lengthening, but data is from a single research group and lacks independent replication.

## The Cancer Paradox

This is the central unresolved tension in telomerase-based longevity research:

- **Cancer hallmark:** Telomerase reactivation is observed in ~85–90% of human cancers, enabling unlimited replicative potential (a hallmark of cancer per Hanahan & Weinberg).
- **Aging hallmark:** Telomere shortening is a hallmark of cellular aging.
- **The dilemma:** Restoring telomerase to "youthful" levels could theoretically delay aging, but chronic or excessive activation might promote malignancy in cells with existing oncogenic mutations.

> **Key unresolved questions:**
> - Is transient, intermittent telomerase activation safer than constitutive activation?
> - Can we activate telomerase selectively in healthy tissues without promoting cancer?
> - What is the therapeutic window between "enough" and "too much"?

## Peptides and Compounds that Modulate Telomerase

| Compound | Type | Mechanism | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[epitalon]]** | Peptide (tetrapeptide) | Binds promoter region of telomerase gene; may transactivate TERT | Low (single-group human data) |
| **TA-65 (Cycloastragenol)** | Plant-derived small molecule | Activates telomerase via unknown mechanism | Low (small human studies) |
| **Gene therapy (TERT)** | Viral vector delivery | Constitutive TERT expression | Preclinical only (mice) |

## Safety Considerations

- Any compound or intervention that activates telomerase carries a **theoretical cancer risk**.
- No long-term human safety studies exist for any telomerase activator.
- The risk/benefit profile differs dramatically between:
  - **Short-term, intermittent use** (e.g., 10-day courses)
  - **Chronic long-term administration**
  - **Systemic vs. localized delivery**
