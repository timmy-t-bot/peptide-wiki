# GHK-Cu (Copper Tripeptide-1)

---

## 1. Overview

**GHK-Cu** (glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine-copper) is a naturally occurring copper(II) complex of the tripeptide glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine. It was first isolated from human plasma albumin by Loren Pickart in 1973 and is also found in saliva and urine. The tripeptide itself has the sequence **Gly-His-Lys** and is sometimes referred to as **glycyl-histidyl-lysine**, **growth-modulating peptide**, **Kollaren**, **liver cell growth factor**, or by its INCI cosmetic name **Copper tripeptide-1** (as the copper complex, Prezatide copper).

### Key Facts

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Sequence** | Gly-His-Lys (GHL) |
| **Chemical formula** | C₁₄H₂₄N₆O₄ (free peptide); C₁₄H₂₂CuN₆O₄ (Cu complex) |
| **Molar mass** | 340.38 g/mol (free); ~403.9 g/mol (Cu complex) |
| **CAS** | 49557-75-7 (free); 89030-95-5 (Cu complex) |
| **Plasma levels** | ~200 ng/mL at age 20, declining to ~80 ng/mL by age 60 |
| **Discovery** | 1973, Loren Pickart, from human plasma albumin |
| **Legal status** | Cosmetic ingredient worldwide; not FDA-approved as a drug; sold as a research chemical |

### History

In the 1960s–70s, Pickart observed that liver tissue from patients aged 60–80 had elevated fibrinogen production. When older liver cells were incubated with plasma from younger individuals, the cells began functioning similarly to younger tissue. This led to the isolation of a small tripeptide factor — GHK — which appeared to chelate metal ions and facilitate copper transport into cells. Since the 1980s, GHK-Cu has been investigated for wound healing, tissue remodeling, anti-aging, and gene-regulatory effects. It has been widely used in cosmetic products for decades.

### Legal Status

GHK-Cu is **not FDA-approved** as a therapeutic drug. It is legally sold as a cosmetic ingredient (INCI: Copper tripeptide-1) in topical formulations worldwide. As a research peptide for systemic use, it falls into a regulatory gray zone — it is not a controlled substance but is not approved for human injection or ingestion. It is available for purchase as a "research chemical" from various peptide vendors.

## 2. Mechanism of Action

### Primary Target: Copper Delivery and Gene Regulation

GHK-Cu operates through a **dual mechanism**:

#### A. Copper Chaperone / Delivery Function

The GHK tripeptide has an exceptionally high affinity for copper(II) ions (stability constant log₁₀ = **16.44**), comparable to the high-affinity copper transport site on plasma albumin (log₁₀ = 16.2). Critically, when copper(II) is bound to GHK, its **redox activity is silenced** — GHK-Cu delivers copper in a non-toxic, bioavailable form. The small size of the tripeptide (~340 Da) allows rapid diffusion through the extracellular space and easy access to cellular receptors.

The molecular structure has been characterized by X-ray crystallography, EPR, NMR, and XAS. The Cu(II) ion is coordinated by:
- Nitrogen from the imidazole side chain of histidine
- Nitrogen from the alpha-amino group of glycine
- Deprotonated amide nitrogen of the Gly-His peptide bond
- Oxygen from the carboxyl group of lysine (from a neighboring complex)
- Apical oxygen from another lysine carboxyl group (square-planar pyramid configuration)

At physiological pH, GHK can readily acquire copper from albumin and transfer it into cells. Copper is essential for ~12 cuproenzymes, including cytochrome c oxidase (respiration), superoxide dismutase (antioxidant defense), lysyl oxidase (connective tissue formation), tyrosinase (melanin production), and ceruloplasmin.

#### B. Gene Expression Modulation

Since 2010, the Broad Institute's Connectivity Map (CMap) has revealed that GHK modulates expression of a **remarkably large fraction of human genes**. At ≥50% change, GHK affects approximately **31.2% of all human genes** (~4,100+ genes), with 59% upregulated and 41% downregulated. Key pathways affected:

- **TGF-β pathway** — upregulation of tissue remodeling genes
- **NFκB pathway** — suppression of p65 and p38 MAPK, reducing inflammatory signaling
- **Caspase genes** — upregulation of apoptosis-related genes (caspases 3, 7, and associated)
- **Ubiquitin/Proteasome System (UPS)** — GHK stimulates 41 UPS genes while suppressing only 1
- **DNA repair genes** — 84 genes associated with DNA repair are affected
- **Wnt/β-catenin pathway** — implicated in hair follicle and skin stem cell regulation

#### Downstream Effects

GHK-Cu's copper delivery and gene regulation converge on multiple downstream pathways:

| Pathway/Effect | Direction | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Collagen synthesis** (types I, IV) | ↑ | Wound healing, skin firmness |
| **Elastin production** | ↑ | Skin elasticity |
| **Glycosaminoglycans** (decorin, etc.) | ↑ (biphasic) | Dermal matrix integrity |
| **VEGF, BDNF, BMP-2** | ↑ | Angiogenesis, neuroprotection, bone |
| **MMP-2, MMP-9** | ↑ (with TIMP regulation) | Balanced tissue remodeling |
| **SOD, glutathione, ascorbic acid** | ↑ | Antioxidant defense |
| **TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β** | ↓ | Anti-inflammatory |
| **Fibrinogen (β-chain)** | ↓ (4.75-fold) | Cardiovascular risk reduction |
| **SIRT1/STAT3** | Modulated | Gut barrier protection (colitis model) |
| **PRDX6** | Targeted binding | Anti-oxidative stress in macrophages |
| **DAF-16/SKN-1** (C. elegans) | Activated | Longevity pathways (FOXO/Nrf2 homologs) |

## 5. Safety Profile

### Known Safety Data

- **Decades of cosmetic use** without reported adverse effects (topical application)
- **Copper redox silencing**: When Cu(II) is bound to GHK, its redox activity is silenced, enabling non-toxic copper delivery
- Bian et al. (2024) explicitly noted GHK-Cu treatment in the silicosis mouse model was **"without significant systemic toxicity"**
- The 2018 Pickart & Margolina review states GHK is "safe, inexpensive, extensively studied" and has been used in humans for decades in cosmetic products without adverse effects

### Contraindications (Theoretical / Known)

- **Wilson disease**: ABSOLUTE contraindication. Wilson disease is a genetic disorder of copper accumulation. GHK-Cu facilitates copper uptake into cells and could exacerbate copper overload.
- **Copper metabolism disorders**: Any condition involving impaired copper homeostasis.
- **Hemochromatosis / iron overload disorders**: GHK-Cu reduces iron release from ferritin (87% reduction shown in vitro), which could theoretically interact with iron metabolism.
- **Pregnancy and lactation**: No safety data exists.
- **Active cancer**: While GHK shows anti-cancer gene profiles and pro-apoptotic effects in some cancer lines, the systemic angiogenic and growth-factor-stimulating properties warrant caution. The data is mixed and insufficient to draw conclusions.

### Known Side Effects

| Side Effect | Context | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Local skin irritation | Topical cosmetic use | Rare / uncommon |
| "Copper uglies" (skin purging) | Anecdotal reports from cosmetic users | Uncommon, transient |
| Injection site reactions | Anecdotal reports from systemic research use | Unknown frequency |
| Copper overload | Theoretical risk with excessive systemic dosing | No documented cases |

### Long-Term Safety

**No long-term systemic safety data exists for injectable or oral GHK-Cu use in humans.** The following remain unknown:

- Carcinogenicity risk with long-term systemic use
- Effects on copper homeostasis with chronic administration
- Reproductive toxicity
- Drug-drug interactions
- Effects in populations with undiagnosed copper metabolism variants
- Cardiovascular outcomes from chronic fibrinogen suppression

### Cancer/Metabolic Risk Assessment

The cancer risk profile of GHK-Cu is **complex and not fully resolved**:

**Potentially protective:**
- Upregulates caspase genes and pro-apoptotic pathways
- Downregulates 70% of metastatic colon cancer gene signatures
- Suppresses NFκB, a pathway associated with many cancers
- Induces apoptosis in neuroblastoma and lymphoma cell lines while sparing healthy fibroblasts
- Suppressed sarcoma-180 in mice (with ascorbic acid)

**Theoretical concerns:**
- Stimulates angiogenesis (VEGF upregulation) — could potentially support tumor vascularization
- Upregulates TGF-β pathway — has context-dependent pro/anti-tumor effects
- Stimulates cell proliferation and tissue remodeling

**Bottom line:** Preclinical data suggests a net anti-cancer profile, but no human cancer outcomes data exists. This should be considered an **unresolved safety question**.

## 6. Dosing Protocols

> ⚠️ **DISCLAIMER:** This information is provided for research and educational purposes only. It is not medical advice. GHK-Cu is not FDA-approved for systemic human use. The protocols below describe what has been used in research settings or reported anecdotally in research communities. No dosing regimen has been validated in human clinical trials.

### Topical (Cosmetic / Research)

Based on clinical studies:

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Concentration | 0.01–100 nM in formulation (cell studies); typically 0.05–2% in cosmetic creams/serums |
| Frequency | Twice daily |
| Duration | 8–12 weeks for measurable effects |
| Delivery | Cream, serum, or liposomal formulation for enhanced penetration |

### Systemic (Research Context Only)

> **The following information is compiled from animal studies and anecdotal reports. There is zero human clinical data on systemic dosing. Any systemic use is experimental and at the user's own risk.**

**Animal dosing (for reference):**

| Route | Dose | Species | Study |
|---|---|---|---|
| Intraperitoneal (IP) | 0.5 μg/kg | Rat | Bobyntsev 2015 (anxiolytic); Sever'yanova 2017 (anti-aggression) |
| IP | 0.5 mg/kg | Mouse | Pickart (anti-pain, hot plate) |
| IP / Intranasal | Not publicly specified in abstract | Mouse | Mazzola et al. 2026 |
| Topical to wound | Various concentrations | Rat, rabbit, pig | Multiple wound healing studies |

**Anecdotal research protocols (unverified, from community reports):**

- Subcutaneous injection: 1–2 mg/day, typically cycled (e.g., 5 days on, 2 off)
- Some protocols combine with BPC-157 or TB-500 for synergistic wound healing

**Critical pharmacokinetic note:** GHK-Cu is rapidly degraded by carboxypeptidases in wound fluid and serum. The half-life in circulation is very short. This limits systemic bioavailability but may not preclude biological effects given the potency at low concentrations.

### Important Cautions

- GHK-Cu is typically supplied as a lyophilized powder requiring reconstitution
- The peptide is sensitive to enzymatic degradation
- Copper content should be calculated when dosing — each molecule carries one Cu(II) ion
- No established therapeutic window exists for systemic use
- No human pharmacokinetic data exists for injectable routes

### Overall Assessment

**GHK-Cu is one of the better-studied peptides in the cosmetic/regenerative space**, with over four decades of research spanning structural chemistry, cell biology, animal models, and limited human clinical trials. Its **topical use for skin health is supported by moderate-quality evidence**. Its **systemic potential** (for inflammation, neuroprotection, longevity, or tissue repair) is **biologically plausible but clinically unvalidated**.

The peptide's ability to modulate ~31% of the human genome is remarkable and suggests broad homeostatic effects, but this breadth also makes mechanistic clarity difficult. The **safety of systemic GHK-Cu is largely unknown**, and copper-related contraindications (especially Wilson disease) are critical.

**Key research gaps:**
1. No human pharmacokinetic data for injectable administration
2. No long-term systemic safety studies
3. No large-scale RCTs for any indication
4. Unresolved cancer risk/benefit profile
5. No established therapeutic window for any systemic indication
