# Thymosin Alpha-1 (Thymalfasin / Zadaxin)

## ⚠️ CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Tα1 is NOT a "Thymosin Beta"

Thymosin alpha-1 (Tα1) and thymosin beta-4 (Tβ4 / [[tb-500]]) share the word "thymosin" in their names but are **chemically, genetically, and functionally unrelated**. Both were isolated from Thymosin Fraction 5 in the 1970s, which is the origin of the shared nomenclature.

| Property | Thymosin Alpha-1 | Thymosin Beta-4 ([[tb-500]]) |
|----------|------------------|------------------------------|
| **Gene** | PTMA (prothymosin alpha) | TMSB4X |
| **Size** | 28 amino acids (~3,108 Da) | 43 amino acids (~4,921 Da) |
| **Sequence** | Ac-SDAAVDTSSEITTKDLKEKKEVEEEAEN | SDKPDMAEI EKFDKSKLKK TETQEKNPLP SKETIEQEKQ AGES |
| **Primary Function** | Immune modulation (TLR agonist) | Actin sequestration + tissue repair |
| **Clinical Evidence** | 30+ trials, 11,000+ subjects, approved in 35+ countries | Phase 1/2 trials only (dry eye, wound healing) |
| **Regulatory Status** | Approved drug in 35+ countries (Zadaxin) | Investigational drug (timbetasin); TB-500 is unregulated research chemical |

## Overview

Thymosin alpha-1 (Tα1) is a 28-amino acid peptide fragment cleaved from prothymosin alpha, encoded by the **PTMA** gene. It was discovered in 1977 by Allan Goldstein as a component of Thymosin Fraction 5 — a crude thymus extract found to restore immune function in thymectomized animals. Tα1 was the first thymosin peptide to be fully sequenced and synthesized.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-wikipedia-2026-06-09.md]

The synthetic pharmaceutical version is called **thymalfasin** (INN) and is marketed under the brand name **Zadaxin** by SciClone Pharmaceuticals. Zadaxin is approved for the treatment of chronic hepatitis B and C and as an immune stimulant/adjuvant in **over 35 countries** worldwide, primarily in China, Southeast Asia, Latin America, the Middle East, and Eastern Europe.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

**Critically, Tα1 is NOT FDA-approved in the United States.** In 2023, the FDA placed Tα1 on a list of peptides restricted from compounding by 503A pharmacies, alongside 21 other peptides. This decision has been contested by researchers who point to the >11,000-subject safety database from clinical trials.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

## Mechanism of Action

### TLR Agonism — The Core Mechanism

Tα1 is an **agonist for Toll-like receptor 2 (TLR2) and Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9)** on both myeloid and dendritic antigen-presenting cells. This is the primary mechanism by which it stimulates the adaptive immune response:^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-wikipedia-2026-06-09.md]

1. **TLR2/TLR9 activation** on dendritic cells triggers maturation and antigen presentation
2. **Increased MHC class-I expression** enhances recognition of infected or malignant cells
3. **Cytokine modulation** — Tα1 shifts the inflammatory balance, increasing Th1-type cytokines (IFN-γ, IL-2) that are critical for antiviral and anti-tumor immunity

### T-Cell Maturation and Immune Restoration

Tα1 was originally identified as the component of Thymosin Fraction 5 that restores immune function in animals without thymus glands. It enhances:^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

- **T-cell differentiation and maturation** — stimulates thymic output, relevant to age-related thymic involution
- **CD4+ T-cell count and CD4+/CD8+ ratio** — consistently demonstrated across multiple meta-analyses (COPD, pancreatitis, sepsis)
- **Dendritic cell and macrophage activity** — enhances antigen presentation and phagocytic function
- **Vaccine response** — improved antibody and T-cell responses in elderly populations

### Anti-Inflammatory Balance

Unlike broad immunosuppressants or broad immunostimulants, Tα1 appears to act as an **immune modulator** — normalizing both deficient and excessive immune responses. In inflammatory conditions (sepsis, pancreatitis, ARDS), it reduces pro-inflammatory cytokines while preserving or enhancing protective immune functions. In immunocompromised states (cancer, chronic viral infection), it enhances the deficient response.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

### Mechanisms Summary

| Mechanism | Evidence Level | Details |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| TLR2/TLR9 agonism | Cell culture + animal | Core mechanism; triggers dendritic cell maturation |
| MHC class-I upregulation | Cell culture + animal | Enhances immune recognition |
| T-cell differentiation | Animal models + human biomarker data | CD4+ increase confirmed in multiple meta-analyses |
| Cytokine modulation | Human trials | Th1 shift; context-dependent anti-inflammatory |
| Chemoprotection | Animal models + human pilot | Reduces chemotherapy toxicity while preserving/boosting anti-tumor immunity |

### Human Clinical Trials

> ✅ Tα1 has one of the **largest clinical evidence bases** of any peptide in the optimization wiki, with >11,000 subjects across 30+ trials. This is a fundamentally different evidence tier from research peptides like [[bpc-157]] or [[ghk-cu]].

#### Hepatitis B and C

**Human evidence: Approved drug in 35+ countries.** The largest and most established indication.

- Tα1 monotherapy is effective in suppressing viral replication in chronic hepatitis B (CHB) compared with untreated controls or conventional interferon.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]
- Combination therapy (Tα1 + lamivudine or IFN-α) shows better effects on HBV DNA suppression and HBeAg seroconversion than either alone.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]
- Phase III trials for hepatitis C with triple combination (IFN + ribavirin + Tα1) were in progress as of 2009.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

#### Cancer Immunotherapy

**Human evidence: Phase 2/3 trials in NSCLC, melanoma.** Tα1 is used as an adjuvant to chemotherapy rather than a standalone treatment.

- **Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC):** Pivotal trial with Tα1 + IFN-α + cisplatin showed evidence of enhanced anti-tumor effect.
- **Stage IV melanoma:** Tα1 + IFN-α + dacarbazine demonstrated benefit; large Phase II dose-finding study conducted.
- Key concept: Tα1 + chemotherapy increases anti-tumor immune response **while reducing chemotherapy toxicity** — a chemoprotective effect.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

#### Sepsis

**Human evidence: 11 RCTs included in 2025 meta-analysis.** Mixed results — the most rigorous sub-analyses suggest insufficient evidence to date.

- Overall meta-analysis: significant reduction in 28-day mortality (OR 0.73, p=0.003)^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]
- **However:** high-quality subgroup (OR 0.82, p=0.09) and multicenter subgroup (OR 0.86, p=0.20) **did not** reach significance. Trial sequential analysis suggests current sample sizes are inadequate.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]
- Potential mortality benefit in subgroups: patients with cancer, diabetes, coronary heart disease (low-to-moderate credibility).

#### Severe Acute Pancreatitis

**Human evidence: 5 RCTs, 706 patients (2025 meta-analysis).** Consistent immune normalization.

- Increased CD4+ T cells and CD4+/CD8+ ratio
- Reduced C-reactive protein (CRP) at lower doses
- Reduced extrapancreatic infections by 44% (RR 0.56)
- No significant reduction in hospital stay^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

#### COPD (Acute Exacerbation)

**Human evidence: 39 RCTs, 3,329 patients (2024 meta-analysis).** Large evidence base — though primarily Chinese trials.

- Significant improvements in FEV1, FEV1/FVC ratio, and arterial blood gases
- Increased CD4+ count and CD4+/CD8+ ratio; decreased CD8+ count
- Shortened hospital stay (mean difference: -5.4 days)^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

#### COVID-19

**Human evidence: Contradictory meta-analyses — CONTESTED.** This is the most actively debated indication.

| Meta-Analysis | Studies | Finding | Source |
|--------------|---------|---------|--------|
| Shang 2023 | 9 studies, 5,352 patients | **No significant effect** on mortality overall (RR 1.03, p=0.92). Subgroup benefit in >60 years, severe/critical disease. | PMID: 36527881 |
| Soeroto 2023 | 8 studies | **Significant mortality reduction** (RR 0.59, p=0.02). No effect on ventilation or hospital stay. | PMID: 37845598 |

The contradiction appears to stem from different study inclusion criteria, different weighting of Chinese vs. non-Chinese studies, and heterogeneity in Tα1 dosing protocols. **Resolution requires larger, well-designed RCTs.** The 2023 Dinetz review concluded Tα1 was effective for COVID-19 based on their broader evidence synthesis, but neither meta-analysis can be considered definitive.

#### Aging / Immunosenescence

**Human evidence: Supported by mechanistic rationale and elderly vaccine studies.** A 2025 review highlights:

- Tα1 may counteract age-related thymic involution and immune decline
- Improved vaccine response in elderly populations
- Anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties relevant to inflammaging
- The fusion drug Refnot (TNFα-Tα1) is being explored for anti-aging applications^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

## Safety Profile

### Clinical Trial Safety Data (Robust)

Tα1 has one of the most extensive safety databases among peptides in the optimization wiki: **>30 clinical trials, >11,000 human subjects.** Across this body of evidence, Tα1 is consistently reported as **well-tolerated** with a favorable safety profile.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

**Common side effects (from clinical reviews):**

- Injection site reactions (mild, transient)
- Mild flu-like symptoms
- Fatigue
- Transient fever

**Rare / serious adverse events:**

- Allergic reactions (rare)
- No dose-limiting toxicities identified in clinical trials
- No significant organ toxicity reported

### ⚠️ Safety Flags and Unknowns

1. **Immunostimulation risk:** As an immunomodulator that enhances Th1 responses, there is a **theoretical risk of exacerbating autoimmune diseases** (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, multiple sclerosis). This risk has not been systematically studied. The Dinetz 2024 review reports Tα1 use in autoimmune conditions, but the risk-benefit balance is not established for all autoimmune diseases.

2. **Long-term carcinogenicity:** No long-term (>5 year) cancer surveillance data published. Tα1 is used as a **cancer treatment adjuvant** (which is reassuring), but whether chronic immunostimulation could promote certain cancer types is unknown.

3. **FDA restriction (2023):** The FDA restricted Tα1 from 503A compounding in 2023, citing lack of sufficient safety data for compounding pharmacy production. This restriction is contested by researchers who point to the >11,000-subject safety database.

4. **Pregnancy and lactation:** No data. Contraindicated.

5. **Reproductive safety:** No published data on fertility or developmental effects.

6. **Drug interactions:** Limited formal interaction studies. The theoretical interaction profile includes other immunomodulators, immunosuppressants, and vaccines.

## Dosing Protocols (Research Context)

> **⚠️ For educational purposes only. Not medical advice.** Tα1 is a prescription drug in 35+ countries and should only be used under physician supervision. In the United States, it is NOT FDA-approved. The following summarizes published clinical trial protocols and community-reported practices.

### Clinical Trial Dosing (Established)

| Indication | Dose | Frequency | Duration | Route |
|-----------|------|-----------|----------|-------|
| Chronic hepatitis B | 1.6 mg | 2× per week | 6-12 months | Subcutaneous |
| Cancer adjuvant | 1.6 mg | Varies by protocol | Throughout chemo cycles | Subcutaneous |
| Sepsis (investigational) | 1.6 mg | Daily or every other day | 5-14 days | Subcutaneous |

Standard clinical dose: **1.6 mg subcutaneously**, typically 2× per week. This is the dose used across most published clinical trials and is the approved dosing for Zadaxin.^[raw/articles/thymosin-alpha-1-pubmed-research-2026-06-09.md]

### Community Protocols (Anecdotal, Not Validated)

Tα1 is discussed in some biohacking and longevity communities, typically for immune enhancement and anti-aging. Community-reported protocols differ significantly from clinical protocols:

- **Immune support / "immune reset":** 450–900 µg daily or every other day, cycles of 2–4 weeks
- **Longevity / anti-aging:** Lower doses reported (300–450 µg, 1–2× per week), often cycled
- **Stacking:** Sometimes combined with [[bpc-157]] for recovery or [[epitalon]] for longevity

**These community protocols have no clinical validation.** The clinical evidence is for specific disease indications at the 1.6 mg dose. Extrapolation to lower doses for "optimization" is speculative.

### WADA Status

Thymosin alpha-1 is **not explicitly listed** on the WADA Prohibited List as of 2026. However:
- **S2 (Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances, and Mimetics):** Tα1 is an immunomodulatory peptide that could potentially be classified under this category.
- **S0 (Non-Approved Substances):** In countries where Tα1 is not approved (e.g., US), it could fall under S0 as an unapproved pharmaceutical substance.
- Athletes subject to WADA testing should seek clarification from their national anti-doping organization before using Tα1.

**Important:** Tα1 is chemically and genetically unrelated to thymosin beta-4 ([[tb-500]]), which IS explicitly prohibited under S2. The similar names should not lead to confusion about their legal/anti-doping status.

## Confidence Assessment: MEDIUM

**Rationale for MEDIUM (not LOW):**

1. **Extensive clinical database:** >11,000 subjects, 30+ trials, drug approval in 35+ countries. This is a fundamentally different evidence tier from most peptides in the wiki. The core immunomodulatory mechanism is well-characterized and clinically validated.

2. **Established safety profile:** Multiple reviews consistently report good tolerability. The safety database is one of the strongest among peptides discussed in the optimization space.

3. **TLR mechanism is solid:** TLR2/TLR9 agonism is a well-established molecular mechanism with extensive basic science support.

**Why not HIGH:**

1. **FDA non-approval:** Despite the large international evidence base, the peptide has not cleared FDA review. The 2023 FDA restriction on compounding is a significant regulatory negative signal.

2. **Contradictory COVID-19 data:** Two 2023 meta-analyses reached opposite conclusions on mortality benefit. This raises questions about study quality, heterogeneity, and the conditions under which Tα1 is effective.

3. **Sepsis evidence is fragile:** The 2025 meta-analysis found overall benefit, but the higher-quality subgroup analyses did not reach significance. Trial sequential analysis suggests inadequate sample sizes.

4. **Geographic concentration of evidence:** A significant portion of the clinical trial data (especially COPD, some hepatitis, and COVID-19 studies) comes from Chinese research groups. Independent replication in diverse populations and healthcare systems is needed.

5. **Mechanism for "immune optimization" in healthy individuals is unproven:** The clinical database is almost entirely in diseased populations. Extrapolation to healthy-subject immune optimization is speculative.

### Areas of Higher Confidence

- Tα1 is an effective immunomodulator with TLR2/TLR9 agonism as the core mechanism
- Clinical benefit in chronic hepatitis B and C is well-established (drug approval basis)
- Safety profile is favorable in >11,000 subjects
- Tα1 is chemically and functionally distinct from thymosin beta-4 / [[tb-500]]

### Areas of Lower Confidence

- Efficacy in sepsis — fragile meta-analytic results
- Efficacy in COVID-19 — contradictory meta-analyses
- Efficacy in healthy subjects for "immune optimization" — no data
- Long-term safety (>5 years) of chronic administration
- Whether FDA non-approval reflects safety concerns or commercial/regulatory factors

### Biggest Knowledge Gaps

1. **No large Western Phase 3 trial with FDA-standard design** — the international approval evidence base has not been presented to the FDA
2. **COVID-19 efficacy is unresolved** — contradictory meta-analyses need resolution through large, well-designed RCTs
3. **Sepsis benefit not confirmed in high-quality subgroups** — more multicenter RCTs needed
4. **No dose-response studies for non-hepatitis indications** — optimal dosing for sepsis, pancreatitis, COPD is not established
5. **No studies in healthy populations** — "immune optimization" is speculative
6. **Long-term immunomodulation safety** — chronic TLR agonism effects unknown
