# LL-37 / Cathelicidin

## Summary

LL-37 is the only known human cathelicidin — a 37-amino-acid cationic antimicrobial peptide (sequence: LLGDFFRKSKEKIGKEFKRIVQRIKDFLRNLVPRTES) derived from the CAMP gene product hCAP-18. It is a central component of mammalian innate immunity, expressed in neutrophils, macrophages, epithelial cells, and keratinocytes. Beyond direct microbicidal activity, LL-37 exhibits pleiotropic effects including angiogenesis, wound healing, immune modulation, and — under pathological conditions — pro-inflammatory and pro-tumorigenic activity. Exogenous administration as a therapeutic peptide remains investigational; no approved human drug formulation exists.

---

## Structure & Pharmacology

- **Full name:** Cathelicidin antimicrobial peptide (CAMP) — active fragment LL-37
- **Sequence:** LLGDFFRKSKEKIGKEFKRIVQRIKDFLRNLVPRTES (37 AA)
- **Precursor:** hCAP-18 (18 kDa), cleaved extracellularly by proteinase 3
- **Expression sites:** Neutrophils, macrophages, monocytes, mast cells, dendritic cells, epithelial cells, keratinocytes
- **Induction:** Upregulated by bacterial/viral/fungal/parasitic stimuli and by 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (active vitamin D)
- **Class:** Host defense peptide; amphipathic α-helical antimicrobial peptide

---

## Mechanism of Action

### Antimicrobial Activity
- **Membrane disruption:** As an amphiphilic α-helix, LL-37 disintegrates microbial lipid membranes, acting against bacteria, viruses, and fungi (Zanetti 2004).
- **Biofilm inhibition:** Inhibits formation of Pseudomonas aeruginosa and other bacterial biofilms.
- **Intracellular killing:** Destroys pathogens within phagolysosomes in macrophages.

### Immune Modulation
- **Chemotaxis:** Utilizes formyl peptide receptor-like 1 (FPRL-1) to attract neutrophils, monocytes, and T cells (Chen 2000).
- **Cytokine modulation:** Stimulates dendritic cells and contributes to interferon α/β release when complexed with self-DNA/RNA.
- **Vitamin D link:** CAMP gene expression is a direct target of the vitamin D receptor; vitamin D deficiency reduces LL-37 production (Liu 2006).

### Tissue Repair & Angiogenesis
- **Wound healing:** Stimulates airway epithelial cell proliferation and wound closure (Shaykhiev 2005).
- **Angiogenesis:** Promotes blood vessel formation (Koczulla 2003).
- **Receptor interactions:** Binds EGFR, P2X7, and FPRL-1, mediating cell migration and proliferation.

### Oncologic Duality
- **Anti-cancer potential:** Fragments such as FK-16 induce apoptosis and autophagic cell death in colon cancer cells (Ren 2013).
- **Pro-tumorigenic risk:** Acts as a growth factor for lung cancer cells via EGFR activation (von Haussen 2008). This duality is context-dependent and unresolved.

## Evidence Assessment

### Human Evidence

- **Endogenous / observational only:** No interventional clinical trials of exogenous LL-37 administration in humans have been published as of 2026.
- **Biomarker studies:** Low plasma hCAP18 predicts increased infectious disease mortality in hemodialysis patients (Gombart 2009).
- **Disease associations:**
  - **Rosacea:** Elevated cathelicidin and aberrant processing contribute to skin inflammation (Yamasaki 2007).
  - **Psoriasis:** LL-37 forms immune complexes with self-nucleic acids, driving autoinflammation. LL-37-specific T cells have been identified in moderate-to-severe psoriasis patients.
  - **Alzheimer's disease:** LL-37 binds amyloid-β peptide; chronic infections may modulate AD progression via cathelicidin pathways.
- **Vitamin D supplementation trials:** Indirect evidence — vitamin D increases CAMP/LL-37 expression, but this is not the same as exogenous LL-37 therapy.

### Animal Models

- **Mouse (CRAMP):** Mice express the ortholog CRAMP (cathelicidin-related antimicrobial peptide). CRAMP knockout mice show increased susceptibility to bacterial infection (Gallo 1997). Mouse models support the antimicrobial and wound-healing roles of the cathelicidin family.
- **Rat / other species:** Cathelicidins are conserved across mammals; orthologs have been studied in mice (CRAMP), rats, pigs, cattle, and other species.

### Cell Culture Studies

- Extensive in-vitro data demonstrate microbicidal activity against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, fungi, and enveloped viruses.
- Cytotoxicity to human cells is dose-dependent; selective toxicity for microbes vs. mammalian cells is concentration-dependent.

## Safety Profile

### ⚠️ Critical Safety Flags

1. **Pro-tumorigenic / angiogenesis risk:** LL-37 promotes angiogenesis and directly acts as a growth factor for lung cancer cells via EGFR activation in cell culture (von Haussen 2008). This raises concern that exogenous administration could promote neovascularization or tumor growth in susceptible individuals. The angiogenesis mechanism is shared with [[bpc-157]] and [[ghk-cu]], representing an unresolved class-level risk.

2. **Autoimmune / inflammatory activation:** In psoriasis and rosacea, LL-37 is a driver of autoinflammation. Exogenous administration could theoretically exacerbate autoimmune skin diseases or systemic lupus (given its role in interferon induction via nucleic acid complexes).

3. **Protease sensitivity / bioavailability:** Like all antimicrobial peptides, LL-37 is rapidly degraded by serum proteases. Injectable or topical formulations would require protection, modification (e.g., SAAP-148), or localized delivery. Systemic half-life in humans is unknown.

4. **Dose-dependent cytotoxicity:** In vitro, LL-37 is cytotoxic to mammalian cells at high concentrations. Therapeutic window in humans is uncharacterized.

5. **No human safety data:** Zero clinical trials of exogenous LL-37. All safety inferences are extrapolated from endogenous biology, animal models, or engineered derivatives such as SAAP-148.

6. **Immunogenicity risk:** As a human peptide, LL-37 is a known auto-antigen in psoriasis. Chronic exogenous administration could theoretically trigger or worsen autoimmunity.

## Legal Status

- **United States / EU:** Not FDA or EMA approved. No approved drug formulation exists.
- **WADA:** Not explicitly listed on the Prohibited List, but peptide hormones and growth factors may fall under S2 or S0 depending on interpretation.
- **Research use only:** Available as a research peptide from chemical suppliers; not legal for human consumption.

## Dosing (Research Context)

**For educational purposes only. Not medical advice. No human dosing protocol exists for exogenous LL-37. The following is based on in-vitro concentrations and animal extrapolation.**

- **No established human dose:** Antimicrobial peptides in clinical trials (e.g., omiganan, pexiganan) have used topical formulations. LL-37 itself has not reached human dosing trials.
- **In-vitro active concentrations:** Typically 1–50 µg/mL against bacteria; mammalian cell cytotoxicity appears at higher concentrations (>100 µg/mL in many cell lines).
- **Modified derivatives:** SAAP-148 (engineered LL-37 variant) has shown enhanced activity in preclinical models but has not reached human trials.

## Confidence Assessment: LOW

**Rationale:**
- Zero interventional human trials of exogenous LL-37
- Robust preclinical data (cell culture, animal models) for antimicrobial and wound-healing effects
- Strong endogenous human biology evidence (biomarker studies, disease associations)
- Oncologic duality (anti-cancer fragments vs. pro-tumorigenic full-length peptide) is unresolved
- Bioavailability and pharmacokinetic data in humans are absent
- No regulatory pathway or approved indication exists

---

## Gaps in Knowledge

1. No human pharmacokinetics or safety data for exogenous administration
2. Therapeutic window (antimicrobial vs. cytotoxic) is uncharacterized in vivo
3. Oncologic safety is unresolved — pro-tumorigenic risk vs. anti-cancer fragments
4. Optimal route of administration (topical, local injection, systemic) is unknown
5. No drug-drug interaction data
6. Long-term effects of chronic administration are entirely speculative
7. Manufacturing and stability challenges for a cationic peptide are significant
