# Melanotan II

## Summary

Melanotan II is a synthetic cyclic heptapeptide analogue of α-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (α-MSH) that acts as a non-selective melanocortin receptor agonist. It was originally developed by the University of Arizona as a sunless tanning agent, but development was abandoned by Clinuvel Pharmaceuticals due to regulatory restrictions. The compound is unapproved for human use in all jurisdictions, yet it is found on the internet and sold through gray-market channels. It stimulates melanogenesis via MC₁ receptor activation and has documented pro-erectile effects via MC₄ receptor activation.

---

## Structure & Pharmacology

- **Systematic name:** N-acetyl-L-norleucyl-L-α-aspartyl-L-histidyl-D-phenylalanyl-L-arginyl-L-tryptophyl-L-lysinamide, cyclic (2→7)-lactam
- **Sequence:** Ac-Nle-cyclo[Asp-His-D-Phe-Arg-Trp-Lys]-NH₂
- **Molecular formula:** C₅₀H₆₉N₁₅O₉
- **Molar mass:** 1024.18 g/mol
- **Class:** Melanocortin receptor agonist; α-MSH analogue

---

## Mechanism of Action

- **Receptor profile:** Non-selective agonist of melanocortin receptors MC₁, MC₃, MC₄, and MC₅ (Wikberg 2001).
- **Melanogenesis:** Activation of MC₁ receptors on melanocytes stimulates eumelanin production, producing skin darkening independent of UV exposure.
- **Sexual arousal:** MC₄ receptor activation is thought to mediate pro-erectile and libido-enhancing effects observed in animal and early human studies (King 2007; Norris & Lopez 2010).
- **Metabolic effects:** MC₄ activation may suppress appetite, contributing to anorectic effects (Plant & Zeleznik 2014).
- **Adverse effects:** Flushing, nausea, vomiting, stretching, yawning, and loss of appetite are also mediated via melanocortin receptor activation (Plant & Zeleznik 2014; Wein 2011).

## Evidence Assessment

### Human Studies

- **Early tanning trials (1990s–2000s):** University of Arizona researchers conducted human studies demonstrating tanning efficacy. These were not formal Phase 3 trials and did not lead to regulatory submission.
- **Sexual dysfunction research:** Early human studies documented increased sexual arousal. One investigator accidentally administered a double dose and experienced an eight-hour erection with nausea and vomiting (King 2007). Palatin Technologies licensed the compound for sexual dysfunction but discontinued development in 2000 in favor of bremelanotide.
- **No approved indication:** Melanotan II has never been approved by the FDA, EMA, or any major regulator. The related compound afamelanotide (Melanotan I) was FDA-approved in 2019 for erythropoietic protoporphyria, but this is a distinct molecule.

### Animal Models

- **Rodent sexual behavior:** α-MSH and analogues induced sexual arousal in rats dating back to the 1960s (Hadley 2005).
- **Melanoma suppression (mouse):** A 2020 in vivo study found that Melanotan II suppressed melanoma progression via PTEN upregulation and COX-II inhibition (Wu 2020). **Caution:** This is a single preclinical study and does not establish safety in humans.

### Anecdotal / Community Evidence

- Extensive anecdotal reporting in bodybuilding, tanning, and biohacking communities.
- Users typically report rapid tanning, darkening of existing moles, increased libido, and nausea.
- The gray-market product is frequently mislabeled or contaminated; health agencies have documented adverse events.

## Safety Profile

### ⚠️ Critical Safety Flags

1. **Melanoma risk ambiguity:** Melanotan II stimulates melanocytes and causes reversible darkening of moles and freckles. Whether this increases melanoma risk is unresolved. Reports of melanoma in users have coincided with heavy UV/sunbed exposure, confounding causal attribution (Javed 2013; Wensink 2021). A single 2020 preclinical study suggested melanoma suppression (Wu 2020), but this does not resolve human safety concerns.

2. **Cardiovascular / hemodynamic effects:** Facial flushing is common. Theoretical concern for hemodynamic effects via melanocortin receptors, though systematic cardiovascular safety data are absent.

3. **Prolonged erection (priapism risk):** Documented in early human studies. This can cause penile tissue damage if sustained.

4. **Nausea and gastrointestinal distress:** Very common adverse effect, mediated by MC₄ activation.

5. **Unregulated product quality:** Unregulated products are not legal to sell for human consumption in any jurisdiction. Health agencies in Denmark, UK, Ireland, Norway, and the US have issued public warnings against Melanotan II use since 2007.

6. **Psychiatric / behavioral effects:** Appetite suppression and potential mood alterations are reported anecdotally. No formal psychiatric safety studies exist.

7. **Pregnancy / lactation:** Absolutely contraindicated due to complete absence of reproductive safety data and melanocortin pathway involvement in fetal development.

---

## Legal Status

- **United States / EU / Australia:** Not approved for human use. FDA and several international health agencies have issued public warnings against sales and use.
- **WADA:** Not explicitly listed on the Prohibited List, but melanocortin analogues may fall under S0 (unapproved substances).
- **General:** Multiple health agencies (Danish Medicines Agency, MHRA, Irish Medicines Board, Norwegian Medicines Agency) have issued public warnings against Melanotan II use.

## Dosing (Research Context)

**For educational purposes only. Not medical advice. Melanotan II is unapproved for human use in all jurisdictions and has significant safety unknowns.**

- **Typical subcutaneous dose (anecdotal):** 0.25–1 mg, often titrated from low doses to assess tolerance
- **Frequency:** Daily or every other day until desired tan achieved, then maintenance 1–2× weekly
- **Reconstitution:** Lyophilized powder with bacteriostatic water; subcutaneous injection
- **UV exposure:** Users frequently combine with sunbed or natural sun exposure, compounding skin cancer risk

**No clinical trial has validated any dosing protocol for safety or efficacy in healthy humans. Dosing information below is derived from anecdotal community reports, not from the peer-reviewed literature.**

---

## Confidence Assessment: LOW

**Rationale:**
- No FDA or EMA approval; development was abandoned by the originator company
- Human evidence consists of small, early-phase studies from the 1990s–2000s, not rigorous RCTs
- Animal data are robust for tanning and sexual effects, but translation to long-term human safety is poor
- Melanoma risk remains unresolved after two decades of use
- Gray-market sourcing introduces purity, sterility, and dosing accuracy risks
- No long-term safety data (>12 weeks) exist

---

## Gaps in Knowledge

1. No large-scale RCTs evaluating tanning efficacy vs. placebo
2. Long-term dermatological safety (melanoma, non-melanoma skin cancer) is unresolved
3. No formal cardiovascular safety assessment
4. Pharmacokinetics in humans are poorly characterized
5. Interaction potential with PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) and other erectile dysfunction agents is unknown
6. Reproductive safety data are absent
7. No regulatory-quality manufacturing standards exist for gray-market products
