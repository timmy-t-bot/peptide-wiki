# MOTS-c

**MOTS-c** (Mitochondrial Open Reading Frame of the 12S rRNA-c) is a 16-amino-acid endogenous peptide encoded by the mitochondrial **MT-RNR1** gene. Its amino acid sequence is **MRWQEMGYIFYPRKLR**. Discovered in 2015 by Changhan Lee and the Pinchas Cohen laboratory at USC, MOTS-c demonstrated that mitochondria, beyond their bioenergetic role, actively signal to the nucleus and other cells via peptides encoded in their own genome.

- **Sequence:** H-Met-Arg-Trp-Gln-Glu-Met-Gly-Tyr-Ile-Phe-Tyr-Pro-Arg-Lys-Leu-Arg-OH
- **Molecular Formula:** C₁₀₁H₁₅₂N₂₈O₂₂S₂
- **Molecular Weight:** 2,174.62 g/mol
- **CAS Number:** 1627580-64-6
- **PubChem CID:** 146675088
- **Gene:** MT-RNR1 (mitochondrially encoded 12S rRNA)
- **Class:** Mitochondrial-derived peptide (MDP)
- **Origin:** Discovered 2015 at USC Davis School of Gerontology (Lee, Cohen et al.)
- **Status:** Research chemical. **Not FDA-approved** for any medical indication. **Banned by WADA** explicitly from 2024 under S0 (non-approved substances). Sold via gray-market research chemical vendors.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]
- **Key caveat:** No human clinical trials have administered exogenous MOTS-c. All human data are correlational/observational. All efficacy data are from animal and cell models.

## Overview

MOTS-c is one of three known classes of mitochondrial-derived peptides (MDPs), alongside **humanin** (HN) and **small humanin-like peptides 1-6** (SHLP1-6). These MDPs are encoded by short open reading frames (sORFs) within mitochondrial DNA using a genetic code distinct from nuclear DNA.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

MOTS-c is considered an **exercise mimetic**: it is upregulated in skeletal muscle and circulation in response to exercise, and its exogenous administration recapitulates many metabolic benefits of physical activity in animal models. Its discovery fundamentally expanded the understanding of mitochondrial function — from passive energy producers to active signaling organelles that communicate with the nuclear genome.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

The peptide has attracted intense research interest (242 PubMed-indexed papers as of June 2026, from just 4 in 2015), but remains **entirely preclinical** for exogenous use. All claims of health benefits in humans are extrapolations from animal models and correlational human biomarker studies.

## Mechanism of Action

MOTS-c acts through multiple intersecting pathways, with its primary mechanism involving metabolic reprogramming via the folate cycle and AMPK.

### Primary Pathway: Folate Cycle Disruption → AMPK Activation
MOTS-c translocates to the nucleus and disrupts the **folate-methionine cycle** by inhibiting folate-dependent de novo purine biosynthesis. This leads to accumulation of **AICAR** (5-aminoimidazole-4-carboxamide ribonucleotide), which directly activates **AMPK** (AMP-activated protein kinase) — the master metabolic sensor and regulator of energy homeostasis.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

AMPK activation drives:
- Increased glucose uptake (GLUT4 translocation) in skeletal muscle
- Enhanced fatty acid oxidation
- Improved mitochondrial biogenesis and function
- Suppressed mTOR signaling (mimicking caloric restriction effects)

### Casein Kinase 2 (CK2) Binding
MOTS-c directly binds to **casein kinase 2** (CK2), identified as a functional target. CK2 is a pleiotropic kinase involved in cell cycle regulation, DNA repair, and circadian rhythm — providing an additional node through which MOTS-c influences cellular physiology.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### Nuclear Retrograde Signaling
MOTS-c translocates to the nucleus under stress conditions and regulates the expression of genes containing **antioxidant response elements (ARE)**. This represents a form of **mitochondrial retrograde signaling** — the mitochondrion communicating with the nucleus to coordinate stress responses.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### PGC-1α and Mitochondrial Bioenergetics
MOTS-c enhances skeletal muscle mitochondrial bioenergetic performance in a manner dependent on both **PGC-1α** (the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis) and **AMPK**. Critically, this occurs without increasing mitochondrial respiratory protein content — suggesting intrinsic improvements in mitochondrial quality and efficiency rather than increased mitochondrial mass. MOTS-c also **reduces mitochondrial ROS emission** and oxidative protein damage.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### Mitophagy (PINK1/Parkin Pathway)
Under mitochondrial stress conditions (e.g., hypoxia, high altitude), MOTS-c activates **PINK1/Parkin-dependent mitophagy** — the selective degradation of damaged mitochondria. Silencing Pink1 abolishes MOTS-c's protective effects, confirming pathway specificity.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### Anti-Ferroptosis (SLC7A11)
MOTS-c targets **SLC7A11**, a key component of the cystine/glutamate antiporter (system Xc⁻), suppressing ferroptosis — an iron-dependent form of regulated cell death. This mechanism has been demonstrated in spermatogenesis protection.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### Summary of Signaling Nodes
| Pathway | Effect | Evidence |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Folate-AICAR-AMPK | Metabolic reprogramming, insulin sensitization | Mouse, cell |
| CK2 binding | Pleiotropic kinase modulation | Cell (in vitro) |
| Nuclear ARE genes | Antioxidant/stress gene expression | Cell, mouse |
| PGC-1α/AMPK | Mitochondrial quality control | Mouse |

## Research Evidence

### Cell Culture Studies
- MOTS-c inhibits the folate cycle and de novo purine synthesis in cultured cells, leading to AICAR accumulation and AMPK activation.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]
- Direct binding to CK2 demonstrated in vitro (Kumagai et al., 2023).
- Nuclear translocation visualized under stress conditions.
- Anti-ferroptotic effects via SLC7A11 confirmed in spermatogonia cultures.

### Mouse Model Studies
**Metabolic health (Lee et al., 2015 — discovery paper):**
- MOTS-c treatment prevented **age-dependent and high-fat-diet-induced insulin resistance** in mice.
- Prevented **diet-induced obesity**.
- Primary target organ: skeletal muscle.

**Physical performance and aging (Reynolds et al., 2021):**
- MOTS-c significantly enhanced physical performance in young (2 mo), middle-age (12 mo), and **old (22 mo)** mice.
- **Late-life-initiated** intermittent MOTS-c treatment (3×/week, starting at 23.5 months) increased physical capacity and extended healthspan.
- Regulation of nuclear genes involved in metabolism and proteostasis.

**Mitochondrial bioenergetics (Gudiksen et al., 2026):**
- Using **two distinct transgenic mouse strains**, MOTS-c augmented muscle mitochondrial performance via PGC-1α and AMPK — with reduced mitochondrial ROS emission.

**Cardiac protection:**
- **Ischemia-reperfusion injury** (rat Langendorff): MOTS-c 0.5 mg/kg → **73% reduction in infarct size**, improved hemodynamics, reduced apoptosis (caspase 3/7/9, BAX down; BCL2, GPX4, FOXO up), enhanced antioxidant defenses. Molecular docking predicted high-affinity interactions with MAPK, mTOR, AMPK, NRF2, PI3K, caspase 3 (Santhanam et al., 2026).^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]
- **High-altitude cardiac dysfunction**: MOTS-c deficiency persisted after return to low altitude; exogenous MOTS-c activated PINK1/Parkin mitophagy to restore function (Feng et al., 2026).^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]
- **Diabetic myocardial fibrosis**: MOTS-c attenuated fibrosis via THBS1/TGF-β pathway inhibition (Li et al., 2026).^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

**Neuroprotection:**
- **Sepsis-associated encephalopathy** (mouse LPS model): MOTS-c 20 mg/kg 4 hours before LPS → reduced mortality, preserved blood-brain barrier integrity, downregulated neuroinflammation, upregulated neurotrophic factors (Bai et al., 2026).^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]
- **Autism model** (rat VPA): MOTS-c 0.5 mg/kg/day from PND 21-46 reversed sociability deficits, repetitive behaviors, and cerebellar Purkinje cell loss. Notably, MOTS-c reportedly **does not cross the BBB** — its neuroprotective effects may be indirect (Güvenir Seven et al., 2026).^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

**Spermatogenesis:**
- MOTS-c ameliorated spermatogenic impairment from mechanical stress (microgravity model) by suppressing oxidative stress and ferroptosis via **SLC7A11** (Liu et al., 2026).^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### Rat Model Studies
- Ex vivo Langendorff heart model (Santhanam et al., 2026): 0.5 mg/kg optimal dose for cardioprotection.
- VPA autism model (Güvenir Seven et al., 2026): behavioral and histological improvements.

### Human Evidence

> **⚠️ Important: ZERO human interventional trials of exogenous MOTS-c exist.** All human data below are correlational/observational biomarker studies or exercise intervention studies measuring endogenous MOTS-c. NOBODY has administered MOTS-c to humans in a clinical trial setting.

**Exercise and MOTS-c (Dieli-Conwright et al., 2021):**
- 49 breast cancer survivors (25 Hispanic, 24 Non-Hispanic White). 16-week aerobic + resistance exercise RCT.
- MOTS-c levels **increased significantly** in Non-Hispanic White BCS post-exercise (p<0.01) but **not in Hispanic BCS**.
- Post-exercise MOTS-c correlated with reduced fat mass, body weight, HOMA-IR, CRP, and increased lean mass.
- First demonstration of **ethnic-specific differences** in MOTS-c response, potentially due to mtDNA variations.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

**Obesity (Yoon et al., 2025):**
- 22 lean vs 32 obese adults scheduled for bariatric surgery.
- Circulating MOTS-c was **higher**, not lower, in obese individuals (273±56 vs 223±50 pg/mL, P<0.01).
- BMI and HOMA-IR independently predicted elevated MOTS-c.
- Biphasic relationship: MOTS-c rose sharply above HOMA-IR of ~6.6 mmol/L·µU/mL.
- MOTS-c remained unchanged 6 months post-bariatric surgery despite significant weight loss.
- Interpretation: elevated MOTS-c in obesity may represent a **compensatory response** to insulin resistance.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

**Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS):**
- Kutuk et al. (2026): 40 PCOS women had significantly **lower** serum MOTS-c (220.2 vs 498.3 pg/mL, P<0.001) and skeletal muscle MOTS-c (74.2 vs 100.0 AU, P=0.005) vs controls. Inversely associated with total testosterone.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]
- Filibeli et al. (2026): 121 PCOS adolescents vs 125 controls. No significant MOTS-c difference (p=0.059). All participants had wild-type AA genotype for m.1382A>C polymorphism.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]
- Contrasting findings may reflect age differences (adolescents vs adults) or disease stage.

**Peritoneal Dialysis (Musolino et al., 2026):**
- 32 PD patients. Urinary MOTS-c inversely correlated with systemic oxidative stress (AOPPs). Dialysate MOTS-c inversely correlated with arterial stiffness (PWV, R=-0.717, p=0.019).
- Proposed a "Mitochondrial-Vascular Axis" in uremia.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

**Male Fertility (Liu et al., 2026):**
- Serum MOTS-c significantly reduced in patients with oligoasthenozoospermia; correlated with semen quality parameters.

**High-Altitude Heart Disease (Feng et al., 2026):**
- Reduced circulating MOTS-c observed in patients with high altitude heart disease and acute coronary syndrome.

**Exercise-induced circulating MOTS-c source (Gudiksen et al., 2026):**
- No arterio-venous difference across the leg during one-legged knee extensor exercise in humans — suggesting skeletal muscle may **not** be the source of exercise-induced circulating MOTS-c.

## Health Optimization Applications

> **For educational purposes only. Not medical advice.** All applications below are hypothetical extrapolations from preclinical data. No human has been administered exogenous MOTS-c in any published clinical trial. The risk profile is entirely unknown.

### Metabolic Health & Body Composition
Preclinical data suggest MOTS-c may improve insulin sensitivity, enhance glucose disposal in skeletal muscle, and prevent diet-induced obesity — effects driven by AMPK activation. However, human biomarker data paradoxically show **elevated** MOTS-c in obesity (possibly compensatory), not a deficiency to be "replaced."^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### Exercise Capacity & Physical Function
The exercise mimetic concept is central: MOTS-c increased physical performance and extended healthspan in old mice, even when initiated late in life (Reynolds et al., 2021). The human exercise data confirm that endogenous MOTS-c increases with training. However, whether exogenous MOTS-c would produce similar benefits in humans — and whether this would be safe — is unknown.

### Longevity & Aging
MOTS-c's effects on AMPK, mitochondrial quality, and healthspan in aged mice position it as a molecule of interest for longevity research. Unlike [[epitalon]], which targets telomerase, MOTS-c targets metabolic and mitochondrial aging pathways. Unlike [[bpc-157]], which promotes tissue repair, MOTS-c acts as a systemic metabolic regulator.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### Cardiovascular Protection
Multiple 2025-2026 animal studies demonstrate cardioprotection — ischemia-reperfusion, high-altitude cardiac dysfunction, diabetic cardiomyopathy. The mechanism involves mitophagy, antioxidant defense, and anti-apoptotic signaling. However, no human cardiac administration data exist.

### Neuroprotection
Animal models of sepsis, autism, and neurodegeneration show neuroprotective effects, but MOTS-c reportedly does not cross the blood-brain barrier — mechanisms may be indirect, limiting direct CNS applications.^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### Fertility
Both human correlational data (reduced in oligoasthenozoospermia) and mouse mechanistic data (anti-ferroptosis via SLC7A11) support a role in spermatogenesis. This is a very new area of investigation (2026).

## Safety Profile

### ⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY WARNING

**MOTS-c has ZERO human safety data.** No clinical trial has ever administered exogenous MOTS-c to humans. The safety profile is completely unknown. The following concerns are theoretical extrapolations from mechanism and animal data.

### Known/Trackable Concerns
1. **No regulatory approval**: Not FDA, EMA, or any major regulatory body approved.
2. **WADA banned**: Explicitly listed on the 2024 WADA Prohibited List under S0 (non-approved substances). Athletes will test positive.
3. **Gray market risks**: Sold via unregulated research chemical vendors. No quality control, no purity verification, no sterility assurance.
4. **AMPK activation in cancer**: AMPK activation has dual roles in cancer — tumor-suppressive in some contexts, tumor-promoting in others. Long-term safety of systemic AMPK agonism is unknown.
5. **CK2 modulation in cancer**: CK2 is a validated oncology target overexpressed in multiple cancers. Chronic exogenous CK2 agonism via MOTS-c is unexplored and could theoretically promote CK2-driven oncogenesis.
6. **Compensatory biology unknown**: Human data show elevated MOTS-c in obesity — what happens when you push this system further with exogenous peptide?
6. **Immunogenicity**: As a foreign peptide (even if endogenous-sequence), repeated injection may trigger anti-drug antibodies.
7. **Mendias & Awan (2026)**: In a comprehensive review of peptide therapies for sports medicine, MOTS-c was flagged as an unapproved peptide where "rigorous human safety data are scarce, and there is potential for serious harm to patients."^[raw/articles/mots-c-sources-2026-06-09.md]

### Unknown Risks
- Long-term effects of chronic AMPK activation
- Off-target CK2 modulation (CK2 is involved in cell cycle, DNA repair, circadian rhythm, and cancer)
- SLC7A11-mediated ferroptosis suppression could theoretically protect cancer cells from ferroptotic death (ferroptosis is a tumor-suppressive mechanism)
- Mitochondrial-nuclear communication disruption
- Effects on developing organisms (contraindicated in pregnancy, unknown effects on fetal development)
- Drug-drug interactions
- Organ-specific toxicity with repeated dosing

### Animal Toxicology
No formal toxicology studies have been published. Rodent studies show no acute adverse effects at tested doses (0.5-20 mg/kg), but chronic toxicology data are absent.

- **Route**: All published research uses intraperitoneal (IP) injection.
- **No oral bioavailability** data exist.
- **Half-life**: Unknown in humans. Animal PK data are limited.
- **Frequency**: Most studies use daily or intermittent (3×/week) dosing.
- **Optimal dose appears study-specific**: 0.5 mg/kg for acute cardioprotection in rats vs 5-20 mg/kg for chronic systemic effects in mice.

### Gray Market Dosing (Anecdotal — NOT RECOMMENDED)
Anecdotal reports from online communities describe human self-experimentation at 5-10 mg subcutaneously 2-3×/week, typically in 4-6 week cycles. These reports are **unverified, unmonitored, and carry unknown risks**. No conclusions about safety or efficacy should be drawn from them.

## Confidence Assessment

**Confidence: LOW**

MOTS-c has an unusually strong preclinical foundation for such a young molecule:
- **Mechanism**: Well-characterized (AMPK, CK2, PGC-1α, PINK1/Parkin, SLC7A11) — multiple independent labs.
- **Animal efficacy**: Robust and replicated across metabolic, cardiac, neurological, and aging models.
- **Growing research volume**: 242 papers in 11 years, accelerating sharply (29 in first half of 2026 alone).

However, confidence remains **low** because:
1. **Zero human exogenous MOTS-c trials**. This is the critical gap. All "human evidence" is observational — measuring endogenous levels, not testing exogenous administration.
2. **Human biomarker data are inconsistent**: MOTS-c is elevated in obesity but reduced in PCOS; exercise increases it in some ethnicities but not others.
3. **Unknown safety profile**: No toxicology, no PK/PD in humans, no adverse event monitoring.
4. **Gray market self-experimentation** is proceeding without any safety data — creating a dangerous data-practice gap.
5. **Biological complexity**: Compensatory upregulation in metabolic disease suggests a regulatory system we don't fully understand; exogenous administration could have paradoxical effects.

Until human Phase 1 safety trials are conducted, MOTS-c must be considered a **research-only compound with completely unknown human risk-benefit profile**.
