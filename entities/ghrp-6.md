# GHRP-6

## Overview

Growth hormone-releasing peptide 6 (GHRP-6; developmental code SKF-110679), also known as growth hormone-releasing hexapeptide, is one of several synthetic met-enkephalin analogues that include unnatural D-amino acids. It was developed as a growth hormone secretagogue (GHS) and acts as a potent stimulator of GH release through activation of the ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a). Unlike GHRH analogues, GHRP-6 shares no sequence relation with GHRH and functions through a completely different receptor. ^[raw/articles/ghrp-research-2026-06-09.md]

**Amino acid sequence:** His-D-Trp-Ala-Trp-D-Phe-Lys-NH₂

**Key identifiers:** CAS 87616-84-0 | Formula C₄₆H₅₆N₁₂O₆ | MW 873.03 g/mol | PubChem CID 5486806

GHRP-6 lacks opioid activity despite its met-enkephalin derivation but is a potent, specific GH releaser. It has very poor oral bioavailability (<1%) and an elimination half-life of approximately 2.5 hours in humans. ^[raw/articles/ghrp-research-2026-06-09.md]

**Current legal/research status:** Not approved as a pharmaceutical drug in any jurisdiction. No ATC code. WADA-prohibited substance (Class S2). Sold as a "research chemical" in gray markets. Wikipedia article flagged for lacking inline citations and disputed factual accuracy (2013).

## Mechanism of Action

GHRP-6 binds to and activates the **ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a)**, a G-protein-coupled receptor distinct from the GHRH receptor. This receptor was originally called the growth hormone secretagogue receptor but was renamed after the discovery of ghrelin as its endogenous ligand. ^[raw/articles/ghrp-research-2026-06-09.md]

Key mechanistic features:

- **Non-GHRH pathway:** GHRP-6-induced GH secretion is exerted through a non-GHRH-dependent mechanism. Demonstrated by Peñalva et al. (1993; PMID: 8421084): GHRP-6 + GHRH produced synergistic GH release (AUC 4412 vs. 973 for GHRP-6 alone), indicating distinct receptor pathways.
- **Somatostatin dependence:** GHRP-6-induced GH secretion is dependent on endogenous somatostatinergic tone. Atropine (which enhances somatostatin tone) completely prevented GH response. Pyridostigmine and insulin-induced hypoglycemia (which inhibit somatostatin) enhanced the response. However, GHRP-6 does not appear to act by changing hypothalamic somatostatin release itself (Peñalva et al., 1993; PMID: 8421084).
- **Arcuate nucleus activation:** GHRP-6 activates orexigenic NPY/AgRP neurons in the hypothalamic arcuate nucleus. Intranasal GHRP-6 in mice activated neurons expressing Ghsr mRNA (63.5%), Agrp mRNA (79%), and Ghrh mRNA (11.4%) (Poelman et al., 2025; PMID: 39813130).
- **Appetite stimulation:** The strongest among GHRPs — reliably induces hunger and increases food intake by increasing both meal frequency and meal size (Poelman et al., 2025; PMID: 39813130).
- **Insulin interaction:** Co-administration with insulin increases GH response to GHRP-6. Conversely, carbohydrate and/or dietary fat consumption around the administration window significantly blunts GH release.
- **Tissue-protective signaling:** Emerging evidence for GHRP-6 activating tissue-protective pathways beyond GH release, including mTOR-P70 pathway activation in renal tubular epithelial cells (Zhao et al., 2025; PMID: 41327290) and anti-inflammatory effects in lung tissue (Wang et al., 2026; PMID: 41534456).

## Research Evidence

### Cell/In-Vitro Studies

- **miRNA regulation of GH synthesis:** miR-709 inhibits GHRP-6-induced GH synthesis by targeting PRKCA in pituitary GH3 cells (Cheng et al., 2020; PMID: 32084499).
- **CD36 receptor ligands:** Aza-isotryptophan analogs of GHRP-6 demonstrated activity as CD36 receptor modulators, suggesting potential applications beyond GH release (Wei et al., 2025; PMID: 41151018).
- **CD36-mediated autophagy:** CD36 ligand (derived from GHRP-6 analog MPE-001) protected retinal pigment epithelial cells from oxidative stress via autophagy — potential relevance to age-related macular degeneration (Dorion et al., 2021; PMID: 33854697).

### Animal Models (Mouse/Rat)

**Shown in mouse models:**

- **Acute lung injury / pulmonary fibrosis:** GHRP-6 reduced neutrophilic alveolitis, attenuated lung compliance failure, improved alveolar-capillary permeability, and reduced IL-1β in acute LPS model. In chronic model (28 days), GHRP-6 preserved lung parenchymal integrity with reduced collagen accumulation. First assessment of pneumoprotective effects of GHRP-6 (Wang et al., 2026; PMID: 41534456).
- **Acute kidney injury:** Self-assembling GHRP-6 peptide hydrogel activated mTOR-P70 pathway, enhanced renal tubular epithelial cell survival, and reprogrammed metabolism (spermidine, L-glutamine, acetyl-CoA enrichment) in ischemic AKI model (Zhao et al., 2025; PMID: 41327290).
- **Intranasal delivery:** Intranasal GHRP-6 (5 mg/kg) increased food intake without adverse effects, activated arcuate nucleus neurons, and elevated serum GH. Ghrelin and MK-0677 did not work via intranasal route (Poelman et al., 2025; PMID: 39813130).
- **Body composition / metabolism:** Mouse study showed significant differences in body composition, muscle growth, glucose metabolism, memory, and cardiac function in mice administered GHRP-6 (cited in Wikipedia; primary source not fully verified).

**Shown in rat models:**

- **Memory consolidation:** The ghrelin receptor antagonist D-Lys-3-GHRP-6 impaired memory consolidation and downregulated hippocampal serotonin HT1A, HT7 receptors and glutamate GluA1 subunit of AMPA receptors — indicating ghrelin signaling role in memory (Beheshti et al., 2020; PMID: 32454141).
- **Estradiol/ghrelin interaction:** Estradiol replacement improved high-fat diet-induced obesity by suppressing ghrelin action in ovariectomized rats — GHRP-6 used as GHSR agonist probe (Yokota-Nakagi et al., 2020; PMID: 32224927).

**Shown in swine models:**

- **Hepatic metabolism:** Ghrelin receptor antagonist [D-Lys3]-GHRP-6 enhanced hepatic fatty acid oxidation and gluconeogenesis in growing pigs, significantly reducing body weight gain and adipose tissue (Zhang et al., 2023; PMID: 37301480).

### Human Studies

**Early GH stimulation studies (1990s):**

- **Oral administration:** Hartman et al. (1992) — 10 normal men. Oral GHRP-6 (300 μg/kg) produced GH responses comparable to IV (1 μg/kg). Peak GH: 18 ± 3.7 μg/L (oral 300 μg/kg) vs. 4.0 ± 1.5 (placebo). GH increase entirely via amplitude increase, not pulse frequency. Duration: 120–150 min. PMID: 1592884.
- **Mechanism study:** Peñalva et al. (1993) — demonstrated synergistic GH release with GHRH, complete blockade by atropine, enhancement by pyridostigmine and insulin-induced hypoglycemia. Established non-GHRH-dependent mechanism. PMID: 8421084.

**Pharmacokinetic study:**
- Cabrales et al. (2013) — 9 healthy male volunteers. Established two-compartment PK model with 2.5h elimination half-life. PMID: 23099431.

**Phase I/II Clinical Trial (2024):**
- Hernández-Bernal et al. (2024) — **Combination EGF + GHRP-6 for acute ischemic stroke.** Phase I/II non-blinded, randomized clinical trial. Safety endpoint: ≤30% serious adverse events with proven causality. This represents the most advanced human clinical development for GHRP-6. Full efficacy results not yet available in abstract. PMID: 38638315.

**Key negative finding:** Despite decades of research, GHRP-6 has not achieved regulatory approval for any indication. No Phase III trials have been completed.

## Health Optimization Applications

GHRP-6 is researched in non-clinical contexts primarily for:

- **GH elevation:** Potent, specific stimulation of endogenous GH release. Synergistic when combined with GHRH analogues.
- **Appetite stimulation:** The strongest appetite-stimulating GHRP — may be researched for contexts where increased caloric intake is desired.
- **Tissue protection/repair:** Emerging animal data on lung protection (ARDS/pulmonary fibrosis), kidney protection (AKI), and metabolic reprogramming. The EGF+GHRP-6 stroke trial represents the most advanced clinical exploration of tissue-protective effects.
- **Body composition:** Theoretical benefits via GH/IGF-1 axis. Mouse data suggest body composition, muscle growth, glucose metabolism, memory, and cardiac function effects.
- **Intranasal delivery potential:** Mouse data show intranasal GHRP-6 effectively engages brain ghrelin signaling — potential CNS applications.

**Important caveat:** All health optimization applications are extrapolated from early human GH stimulation studies, animal models, or one ongoing stroke trial. No human efficacy trials exist for body composition, performance, or anti-aging endpoints.

## Safety Profile

### Known Side Effects

- **Hunger:** Strong, reliable appetite stimulation — a pharmacodynamic effect that may be undesirable depending on context.
- **ACTH/cortisol elevation:** As a non-selective ghrelin receptor agonist, GHRP-6 may stimulate ACTH and cortisol release similarly to other GHRPs, though this has been less systematically characterized than for GHRP-2. Long-term consequences of repeated elevation unknown.
- **Atypical PK spikes:** Concentration spikes observed during elimination phase in 4/9 subjects in PK study (Cabrales et al., 2013) — mechanism and clinical significance unknown.

### Contraindications

- **Active malignancy:** Theoretical concern — GH/IGF-1 axis stimulation could potentially promote tumor growth. No direct evidence with GHRP-6.
- **Cortisol-sensitive conditions:** ACTH stimulation may be problematic.
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** No data. Contraindicated.
- **Disordered eating:** Strong appetite stimulation may be problematic.

### Drug Interactions

- **Carbohydrate/fat consumption:** Blunts GH release from GHSs — administration should be separated from meals.
- **Insulin:** Co-administration increases GH response.
- **GHRH analogues:** Synergistic GH release when combined.
- **Atropine/anticholinergics:** Atropine completely prevented GHRP-6-induced GH secretion (Peñalva et al., 1993; PMID: 8421084).

### Long-Term Safety

**No long-term human safety data exist.** GHRP-6 has no regulatory approval. The longest human exposure data come from the Phase I/II stroke trial (duration unknown from abstract). All long-term safety concerns are theoretical:

- Chronic ACTH/cortisol elevation risks
- GH/IGF-1 axis overstimulation risks (insulin resistance, fluid retention, carpal tunnel, theoretical neoplasm promotion)
- Receptor desensitization with chronic use (unstudied)
- Ghrelin receptor antagonism studies show memory impairment in rats (PMID: 32454141) — whether chronic agonism has opposite or different effects is unknown

### Gray-Market Concerns

As a research chemical sold outside regulated pharmaceutical channels, GHRP-6 products are not subject to quality control. A stability study found that GHRP-6 generates modified species under thermal stress in different pH and buffers (Santana et al., 2021; PMID: 33272786) — storage and handling conditions significantly affect product integrity.

## Dosing Protocols (Research Context)

> ⚠️ **For educational purposes only. Not medical advice.** GHRP-6 is not an approved drug. No established human therapeutic dosing exists. The following reflects research protocols only.

**Human research dosing (published studies):**
- IV bolus: 100–400 μg/kg (Cabrales et al., 2013; PMID: 23099431)
- Oral: 300 μg/kg produced GH responses comparable to 1 μg/kg IV (Hartman et al., 1992; PMID: 1592884) — but oral bioavailability is <1%, requiring much higher oral doses
- GH stimulation testing: 1 μg/kg IV (Peñalva et al., 1993; PMID: 8421084)

**Anecdotal protocols (not clinically validated):**
- Commonly researched at 100–300 μg via subcutaneous injection, 1–3× daily
- Typically administered in fasted state (at least 2 hours after eating, 30+ minutes before eating)
- Frequently combined with GHRH analogues ([[modified-grf-1-29]], [[cjc-1295-dac]]) for synergistic GH pulse
- Pre-bed administration common to leverage natural nocturnal GH pulse
- Cycle durations of 8–12 weeks reported anecdotally

**Intranasal (animal data only):**
- Mouse: 5 mg/kg intranasal effective for food intake and GH elevation (Poelman et al., 2025; PMID: 39813130). No human intranasal data.

**Detection window (anti-doping):**
- GHRP-6 and other GHRPs detectable by LC-HRMS in dried blood spots. Fully automated DBS sample preparation methods validated for 46 lower molecular mass doping agents including GHRPs (Lange et al., 2020; PMID: 32300840).

| Property | GHRP-6 | GHRP-2 | Ipamorelin | Hexarelin |
|----------|--------|--------|------------|-----------|
| **Class** | GHRP (hexapeptide) | GHRP (hexapeptide) | GHRP (pentapeptide) | GHRP (hexapeptide) |
| **GH release potency** | Moderate-High | High | Moderate | Highest |
| **ACTH/cortisol elevation** | Yes | Yes | **None** | Yes |

| **Oral bioavailability** | <1% | Moderate | Limited | Limited |
| **Half-life** | ~2.5 h | ~1.5 h | ~2 h | ~1 h |
| **Tissue-protective data** | **Emerging (lung, kidney)** | Limited (tendon) | Limited | Limited |
| **Clinical development** | Phase I/II (stroke) | Diagnostic only (Japan) | Discontinued | Phase II (some) |
| **Key advantage** | Appetite + tissue protection | Oral activity + potency | Selectivity (no cortisol) | Potency |
| **Key disadvantage** | Strong hunger, no approval | Cortisol elevation | Discontinued | Cortisol elevation |

**vs. GHRP-2:** GHRP-6 has stronger appetite stimulation, longer half-life (2.5h vs. 1.5h), and more tissue-protective research data (lung, kidney). GHRP-2 has better oral bioavailability and slightly higher GH release potency. See [[ghrp-2]].

**vs. Ipamorelin:** GHRP-6 stimulates ACTH/cortisol and produces strong hunger — both absent with ipamorelin. However, GHRP-6 has more extensive research history and emerging tissue-protective data that ipamorelin lacks. See [[ipamorelin]].

**vs. CJC-1295 / Tesamorelin:** GHRP-6 acts through the ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a), while CJC-1295 and tesamorelin act through the GHRH receptor. The two classes are synergistic when combined. See [[cjc-1295-dac]], [[tesamorelin]].

## Confidence Assessment

- **Overall confidence: LOW**
- **Reasoning:** GHRP-6 has a substantial research history spanning 30+ years with well-characterized human GH stimulation pharmacology and pharmacokinetics. However, it has zero regulatory approvals, zero completed Phase III trials, and zero human efficacy data for any health optimization indication. The most advanced clinical trial (EGF+GHRP-6 for stroke, Phase I/II) is still early-stage. Animal tissue-protective data are promising but entirely preclinical. Long-term human safety is completely unknown. The Wikipedia article itself is flagged for disputed factual accuracy.
- **Biggest gaps in knowledge:**
  1. No human efficacy trials for body composition, performance, recovery, or anti-aging
  2. No long-term (>days) safety data in humans
  3. Chronic ACTH/cortisol elevation consequences unknown
  4. Optimal dosing for non-diagnostic purposes unknown
  5. Tissue-protective effects not confirmed in humans (stroke trial ongoing/early)
  6. Receptor desensitization with repeated dosing unstudied
  7. Atypical PK spikes mechanism and significance unknown
  8. Gray-market product quality, stability, and purity unregulated
  9. Intranasal delivery not studied in humans
