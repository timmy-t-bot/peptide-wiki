# GHRP-2 (Pralmorelin)

## Overview

GHRP-2 (INN: Pralmorelin; brand name GHRP Kaken 100; developmental codes KP-102, GPA-748, WAY-GPA-748) is a synthetic hexapeptide growth hormone secretagogue (GHS) and ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a) agonist. It was developed by Polygen (Germany) and Tulane University (Dr. Cyril Bowers) as an analogue of met-enkephalin. It is marketed by Kaken Pharmaceutical in Japan as a **diagnostic agent** for assessment of growth hormone deficiency (GHD) — the first GHS introduced clinically. ^[raw/articles/ghrp-research-2026-06-09.md]

**Amino acid sequence:** D-Ala-D-(β-naphthyl)-Ala-Trp-D-Phe-Lys-NH₂

**Key identifiers:** CAS 158861-67-7 | Formula C₄₅H₅₅N₉O₆ | MW 817.99 g/mol | PubChem CID 6852372

GHRP-2 is orally active and reliably induces sensations of hunger and increases food intake in humans. It was investigated in Phase II trials for short stature (pituitary dwarfism) but was never marketed for therapeutic indications — possibly because GH response is significantly lower in GHD patients than in healthy individuals. ^[raw/articles/ghrp-research-2026-06-09.md]

**Current legal/research status:** Approved in Japan as a diagnostic agent only (GHRP Kaken 100). Not approved as a therapeutic drug in any jurisdiction. WADA-prohibited substance (Class S2: Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances, and Mimetics). Sold as a "research chemical" in gray markets globally.

## Mechanism of Action

GHRP-2 binds to and activates the **ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a)**, a G-protein-coupled receptor expressed in the pituitary and hypothalamus. This is the same receptor activated by endogenous ghrelin and is distinct from the GHRH receptor. ^[raw/articles/ghrp-research-2026-06-09.md]

Key mechanistic features:

- **ACTH/cortisol stimulation:** Unlike the selective GHRP ipamorelin, GHRP-2 stimulates ACTH and cortisol release in addition to GH. This is clinically utilized in Japan where the GHRP-2 test is used to assess both GH and ACTH reserve (Suzuki et al., 2022; PMID: 35795807).
- **Appetite stimulation:** As a ghrelin mimetic, GHRP-2 activates orexigenic NPY/AgRP neurons in the arcuate nucleus, producing reliable hunger sensations.
- **Non-GHRH pathway:** GHRP-2-induced GH secretion is dependent on endogenous somatostatinergic tone but is not mediated through the GHRH receptor — demonstrated by synergistic effects when co-administered with GHRH analogues (mechanism established for GHRP-6 and applicable to the class; Peñalva et al., 1993; PMID: 8421084).

## Research Evidence

### Cell/In-Vitro Studies

- **miRNA regulation:** miR-709 inhibits GHRP-6-induced GH synthesis by targeting PRKCA in pituitary GH3 cells — demonstrates post-transcriptional regulation of GHSR signaling (Cheng et al., 2020; PMID: 32084499). Applicable to GHRP class generally.

### Animal Models (Mouse/Rat)

**Shown in rat models:**
- **Tendon-bone healing:** GHRP-2 associated with decreased M1 macrophage production and increased histologic and biomechanical tendon-bone healing properties in a rat rotator cuff tear model (Li et al., 2025; PMID: 39672241). This is the only tissue-repair study specific to GHRP-2.

**Shown in general pharmacology studies (multiple species):**
- No serious general pharmacological effects at GH-releasing doses in CNS, autonomic nervous system, respiratory, cardiovascular, digestive, renal, or blood systems in animal models (Furuta et al., 2004; PMID: 15646371).

### Human Studies

**Diagnostic use (approved in Japan):**
- GHRP-2 is used clinically in Japan as a provocative GH stimulation test (GHRP2T). The cut-off peak GH level is higher than in other GH stimulation tests because GHRP-2 is a more powerful stimulus (Onuki et al., 2024; PMID: 38958228).
- The GHRP-2 test is considered relatively safe among endocrine stimulation tests for elderly patients and can assess anterior pituitary reserve capacity (Teramoto et al., 2023; PMID: 37295337).
- The GHRP-2 test induces release of both GH and ACTH, making it useful for assessing hypothalamic-pituitary disorders where GHD is frequently accompanied by secondary adrenal insufficiency (Suzuki et al., 2022; PMID: 35795807).

**Clinical development (discontinued):**
- Phase II trials were conducted for short stature (pituitary dwarfism) by Kaken in Japan (KP-102 LN) and for GH deficiency by Wyeth in the US. Both development programs were discontinued. The US program was terminated by Wyeth; the Japanese therapeutic program never reached market (Adis Editorial, 2004; PMID: 15230633).
- The ability of pralmorelin to increase plasma GH levels is significantly lower in people with GHD relative to healthy individuals, which may explain the failure to develop as a therapeutic agent.

**Anti-doping detection:**
- GHRP-2 is detectable in dried blood spots (DBS) by LC-MS/MS up to 4 hours after a single 100 μg IV dose. Detection limit: 50 pg/mL. Long-term stability (>2 years) confirmed in DBS samples (Reverter-Branchat et al., 2021; PMID: 33197153).

**Key negative finding:** No therapeutic indication for GHRP-2 has succeeded in clinical development. The only approved use is as a one-time diagnostic test.

## Health Optimization Applications

GHRP-2 is researched in non-clinical contexts primarily for:

- **GH elevation:** Potent stimulation of endogenous GH release. Oral activity has been demonstrated in the GHRP class, though specific GHRP-2 oral dosing data in humans are limited.
- **Appetite stimulation:** Reliable hunger induction via ghrelin receptor agonism — stronger than ipamorelin, weaker than GHRP-6.
- **Tissue healing:** Emerging animal data on tendon-bone healing (rat rotator cuff model; PMID: 39672241). No human tissue-healing data exist.
- **Body composition:** Theoretical benefits via GH/IGF-1 axis elevation. No human body composition trials exist for GHRP-2.

**Important caveat:** All health optimization applications are extrapolated from diagnostic use data, animal models, or class effects. No human efficacy trials exist for any body composition, performance, or anti-aging endpoint.

## Safety Profile

### Known Side Effects

- **Hunger:** Reliably induces hunger — this is a pharmacodynamic effect, not an adverse event per se, but may be undesirable in some contexts.
- **ACTH/cortisol elevation:** GHRP-2 stimulates ACTH and cortisol release. The long-term consequences of repeated cortisol elevation are unknown.
- **General pharmacology:** Animal studies showed no serious effects on CNS, cardiovascular, respiratory, renal, or blood systems at GH-releasing doses (Furuta et al., 2004; PMID: 15646371).

### Contraindications

- **Active malignancy:** Theoretical concern — GH/IGF-1 axis stimulation could potentially promote tumor growth. No direct evidence with GHRP-2.
- **Cortisol-sensitive conditions:** ACTH stimulation may be problematic in conditions exacerbated by cortisol elevation.
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** No data. Contraindicated.

### Drug Interactions

- **Carbohydrate/fat consumption:** Blunts GH release from GHSs (demonstrated for GHRP-6; class-applicable).
- **Insulin:** Co-administration with insulin increases GH response (demonstrated for GHRP-6; PMID: 8421084).
- **GHRH analogues:** Synergistic GH release when combined with GHRH or GHRH analogues.
- **Atropine/anticholinergics:** Atropine completely prevented GHRP-6-induced GH secretion (Peñalva et al., 1993; PMID: 8421084) — class effect likely applicable.

### Long-Term Safety

**No long-term human safety data exist.** GHRP-2 is approved only for single-dose diagnostic use. No studies have examined repeated administration over weeks or months in humans. All long-term safety concerns are theoretical:

- Chronic ACTH/cortisol elevation risks
- GH/IGF-1 axis overstimulation risks (insulin resistance, fluid retention, theoretical neoplasm promotion)
- Receptor desensitization with chronic use (unstudied)

### Gray-Market Concerns

As a research chemical sold outside regulated pharmaceutical channels, GHRP-2 products are not subject to quality control. Purity, sterility, and accurate dosing cannot be assumed.

## Dosing Protocols (Research Context)

> ⚠️ **For educational purposes only. Not medical advice.** GHRP-2 is not an approved therapeutic drug. No established human therapeutic dosing exists. The following reflects research and diagnostic protocols only.

**Diagnostic use (approved in Japan):**
- Single-dose administration for GH stimulation testing. Specific diagnostic dosing protocols are established in Japanese clinical practice.

**Research/anecdotal protocols (not clinically validated):**
- Commonly researched at 100–300 μg via subcutaneous injection, 1–3× daily
- Often administered in fasted state (carbohydrate/fat intake blunts GH response)
- Frequently combined with GHRH analogues (e.g., [[modified-grf-1-29]], [[cjc-1295-dac]]) for synergistic GH pulse
- Cycle durations of 8–12 weeks reported anecdotally, with breaks between cycles

**Oral dosing:** GHRP-2 has oral activity. Research by Hartman et al. (1992) on GHRP-6 showed 300 μg/kg oral produced GH responses comparable to 1 μg/kg IV. GHRP-2 oral bioavailability is higher than GHRP-6.

**Detection window (anti-doping):** Detectable in blood for approximately 4 hours after 100 μg IV dose (Reverter-Branchat et al., 2021; PMID: 33197153).

## Comparison with Related Peptides

| Property | GHRP-2 | GHRP-6 | Ipamorelin | Hexarelin |

| **Class** | GHRP (hexapeptide) | GHRP (hexapeptide) | GHRP (pentapeptide) | GHRP (hexapeptide) |
| **GH release potency** | High | Moderate-High | Moderate | Highest |
| **ACTH/cortisol elevation** | **Yes** | Yes | **None** | Yes |
| **Prolactin elevation** | None | None | None | Minimal |
| **Appetite stimulation** | Moderate | **Strong** | Mild | Strong |
| **Oral bioavailability** | **Moderate** | <1% | Limited | Limited |
| **Half-life** | ~1.5 h | ~2.5 h | ~2 h | ~1 h |

| **Key advantage** | Oral activity + potency | Appetite + tissue repair data | Selectivity (no cortisol) | Potency |
| **Key disadvantage** | Cortisol elevation | Strong hunger, no approval | Discontinued development | Cortisol elevation |

**vs. Ipamorelin:** GHRP-2 is more potent for GH release but stimulates ACTH/cortisol, which ipamorelin does not. Ipamorelin is preferred in research contexts where cortisol elevation is undesirable. See [[ipamorelin]].

**vs. GHRP-6:** GHRP-2 has better oral bioavailability and slightly higher GH release potency. GHRP-6 has stronger appetite stimulation and more tissue-protective research data (lung, kidney). See [[ghrp-6]].

**vs. CJC-1295 / Tesamorelin:** GHRP-2 acts through the ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a), while CJC-1295 and tesamorelin act through the GHRH receptor. The two classes are synergistic when combined. See [[cjc-1295-dac]], [[tesamorelin]].

## Confidence Assessment

- **Overall confidence: LOW**
- **Reasoning:** GHRP-2 has more human data than many research peptides (approved diagnostic use in Japan, multiple clinical studies on GH stimulation testing), but zero human efficacy data for any therapeutic or health optimization indication. The Phase II development programs for short stature and GH deficiency were both discontinued. No long-term safety data exist beyond single-dose diagnostic use. Animal data are limited to one recent tendon-healing study and general pharmacology screening.
- **Biggest gaps in knowledge:**
  1. No human efficacy trials for body composition, performance, recovery, or anti-aging
  2. No long-term (>single dose) safety data in humans
  3. Chronic ACTH/cortisol elevation consequences unknown
  4. Optimal dosing for non-diagnostic purposes unknown
  5. No studies on combination with GHRH analogues in humans
  6. Receptor desensitization with repeated dosing unstudied
  7. Gray-market product quality and purity unregulated
