# Dihexa — Wikipedia Excerpt

Dihexa (developmental code PNB-0408; also known as N-hexanoic-Tyr-Ile-(6) aminohexanoic amide) is an oligopeptide drug derived from angiotensin IV that binds with high affinity to hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) and potentiates its activity at its receptor, c-Met. The compound has been found to potently improve cognitive function in animal models of Alzheimer's disease-like mental impairment.

## History
Dihexa was derived from structure-activity studies on the precursor compound Nle1-angiotensin IV (Nle1-AngIV), which itself was derived from angiotensin IV, a metabolite of the potent vasoconstrictor peptide angiotensin II. Angiotensin IV had been found to enhance acquisition, consolidation, and recall of learning and memory in animal models when administered centrally (e.g., by intracerebroventricular injection), and was considered a potential drug candidate for dementia. However, angiotensin IV rapidly degrades in plasma, making it unsuitable for oral administration. Additionally, angiotensin IV does not cross the blood-brain barrier, precluding its use in a non-invasive manner. Thus, in order to develop a more practical drug, researchers at Washington State University and the biotechnology company M3 Biotechnology (now Athira Pharma) undertook structure-activity relationship studies to create an orally active, metabolically stable analogue of angiotensin IV with blood-brain barrier permeability. These efforts led to the development of dihexa, which is orally active and crosses the blood-brain barrier. Dihexa binds with high affinity to HGF and potentiates its activity at its receptor, c-Met, and has been found to potently improve cognitive function in animal models of Alzheimer's disease-like mental impairment.
